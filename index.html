The Ulam Spiral

The Ulam Spiral, named after Stanislaw Ulam who discovered this representation of prime numbers in 1963, is a visual depiction of the set of prime numbers. In this visualizer, each number is represented by a point on the spiral. Each 'represented' number has a fixed distance from its neighboring numbers based on the 'n' inputed. If a number is a spiral, it will be marked with a circle.

The magnificence of the Ulam, or prime, spiral is the appearance of numerous vertical, diagonal, and horizontal lines. Looking at a spiral of randomly-generated odd numbers, the unlikely nature of these lines become apparent.

Other than its mind-bending depiction of primes, the Ulam Spiral may even play a large role in several unsolved number theory problems, such as the Twin Prime conjecture, Goldbach's conjecture, and Legendre's conjecture. Of all these problems, I am most familiar with the Twin Prime conjecture which asserts whether there are infinite number of prime numbers, p, such that p + 2 is also prime, forming a pair. Related to this conjecture, is the idea of a prime k-tuple which is a collection of numbers that represent a repeatable pattern of differences, k, between prime numbers. Galvanized by Yitang Zhang and furthered by prominent mathematicians such as Terence Tao and James Maynard, the lowest bound, or k, has been shown to be less than or equal to 246 and even less than or equal to 6 given the assertions of the Elliot-Halberstam conjecture.

Before you try

First of all, thanks for visiting! :)

I developed this page with vanilla JS

Please visit this webpage on a laptop or computer, as I haven't tested out the canvas API on mobile devices given their proportions

Also, with large numbers, it will take some time to calculate the spiral and your device may get a bit laggy, so I recommend visualizing to, at most, 500000
